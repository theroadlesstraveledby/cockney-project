The Great Cockney Project

 

If you've never gotten familiar with Cockney rhyming slang, this site will give you a few examples to some of the more known phrases.

So how does it work? In most cases, each word has a matching rhyme that consists of two words. When used as part of a conversation - the last word of th rhyme is omitted (sometimes the first word is dropped instead).

So, for instance, if you'd like to tell someone to go down the stairs, you might use the phrase "Go down the apples and pears" or simply "Go down the apples".

Cockney rhyming slang emerged not so long ago, around the 1840s in the East End area in London, and slowly spread throughout the city and many other parts of the UK. It remains quite popular to this day.

Here you can find a Wordbook with lots of other Cockney phrases.

 

Hansel and Gretel = Kettle
Example: "Put the hansel on! I'm gasping for a good cuppa!"

Brown Bread = Dead
Example: "Have you heard what happened to Peter? He fell in the lake and drowned. Now he's brown bread!"

King Lear = Beer
Ship Full Sail = Pint of Ale
Example: "I'm dying for a Lear!" Or - "Fancy a sail?"

Baked Bean = The Queen
Example: "God save the Bean."
